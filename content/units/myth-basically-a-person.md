Two opposite mistakes, one fix. Some people talk to it like a colleague. Others wave it off as
autocomplete with good PR. Both get worse results, for the same reason.

## What's actually happening

It has read an enormous amount of text and learned which words tend to follow which. Given what
you've written, it produces what most plausibly comes next.

That's it. That's the mechanism.

::note
"Just autocomplete" is technically close and practically misleading. Autocomplete finishes your
word. This finishes your document, in your register, having noticed what you were driving at.
::

## Why the "it's a person" mistake costs you

If you think it understands, you'll be vague, the way you're vague with a colleague who already has
the context. "Can you tidy this up?" works on a person who was in the meeting. It gets you generic
output here, because ==nothing is implied. Only what you typed exists.==

## Why the "it's a trick" mistake costs you

If you think it's a parlour trick, you'll test it on something trivial, get a trivial result, and
stop. Most people who've decided AI isn't impressive ran exactly one weak experiment.

## The useful posture

Somewhere in between: a very widely read collaborator with no memory of your organisation, no stake
in the outcome, and no ability to ask what you meant.

Which tells you what a good [[prompt]] is. Not magic words. Just ~~the context a new person would
have needed~~.
