Let's start with the one that stops people from touching it at all.

## The part that's true

Some of this fear is correct, and you should not let anyone talk you out of it.

Work that is mostly producing a first draft of something predictable has already changed. Routine
copy, first-pass summaries, boilerplate contracts, the initial version of a slide deck. If your role
is largely that, the ground has moved and pretending otherwise helps nobody.

::note
I'm not going to tell you the fear is irrational. I've watched enough restructures to know better.
::

## The part that isn't

Here's what the fear gets wrong: ==it does tasks, not jobs==.

Think about what you actually did last Tuesday. Not your job title, the hours. Some of it was
producing text. Most of it was deciding what mattered, reading a room, knowing which of two
imperfect options your organisation could actually survive, and following up with the person who
went quiet.

A tool that drafts well touches the first of those and none of the rest.

## Why this matters for what comes next

The people getting displaced fastest aren't the ones whose jobs contained drafting. They're the ones
who ~~didn't know the drafting was the replaceable part~~ and so never moved their weight onto the
rest.

Knowing which parts of your work are which is the actual defence. That's a foundations question,
which is what the rest of this is about.
