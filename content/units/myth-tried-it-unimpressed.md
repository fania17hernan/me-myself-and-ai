This is the quiet one. Nobody announces it in a meeting. But a lot of capable, senior people tried
this once, got something mediocre, closed the tab, and privately filed it under overhyped.

## The part that's true

The mediocre answer was real. You didn't imagine it.

Ask for "a marketing plan" and you get something that reads like it was assembled from every
marketing plan ever written, because that is precisely what happened.

## The part that isn't

The conclusion. ==A vague ask gets a vague answer, and that is not a verdict on the tool.==

Here's the thing about that first attempt: you gave it nothing. No audience, no constraints, no
sense of what your organisation can actually do, no example of what good looks like to you. A new
hire handed the same sentence would produce the same forgettable document.

## What was missing

Four things, usually. Not magic words, just the briefing you'd give a person:

- **Who it's for.** "Our procurement contact at a mid-size hospital," not "customers."
- **What good looks like.** Paste something you liked. One example beats a paragraph of description.
- **The constraints.** Length, tone, what's off the table, what's already been tried.
- **What you actually have.** Your notes, the real numbers, the messy draft. It can't use what it
  can't see.

::note
The gap between a bad result and a good one is almost never the tool. It's how much of your context
made it into the room.
::

## Worth one more try

Take the thing you tried before. Add those four. Same tool, same task, five more minutes of setup.

That's the whole experiment, and it's the last thing Level 0 asks of you.
