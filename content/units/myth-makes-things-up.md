## The part that's true

It does make things up. Confidently, in fluent prose, with no tell.

There's a word for it, ==[[confabulation]]==, and it's the right word. Lying requires knowing the
truth and choosing otherwise. This doesn't know anything. It produces text that fits the shape of an
answer, and sometimes the shape is right while the content is wrong.

If you have ever been handed a citation that didn't exist, your instinct was correct and you should
keep it.

## The part that isn't

The fear says: it's unreliable, therefore it's useless. That doesn't follow, because ==it isn't
uniformly unreliable==. It's unreliable in a pattern you can learn.

It invents most when it knows least: specific numbers, named sources, recent events, anything where
one exact fact is required and the fact is obscure.

It invents least when the work is transformation rather than recall. Rewrite this. Summarise the
document I just gave you. Turn these notes into an outline. Explain this concept three ways. In all
of those, the material is in front of it and there's nothing to fill in.

::note
The unreliability isn't random. That's the whole point. Random would be useless. Patterned is
workable.
::

## The habit

One question, every time: **could I check this in under a minute?**

If yes, use it and check it. If no, that's the work you don't hand over yet.
