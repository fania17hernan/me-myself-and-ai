Three concepts · pick a direction, not a colour

Same content, three philosophies about how much interface should exist while someone is learning.

These are not variations. Each answers a different question about what your learner is doing when they open this, and each makes a real sacrifice to do it well.

Your palette is already right. The Awwwards UI-design pool this month runs on warm reds, burnt orange and ochre with editorial serifs, which is where Barro and Marigold already live. What was wrong with the last mock was that everything sat in a rounded card at one width, so nothing had a hierarchy. All three concepts below fix that differently.

A

The Field Manual

Learning as consulting a beautifully made technical document. Indexed, numbered, precise. Trust comes from evident craft and rigour, not from friendliness.

InspirationAviation checklists, Tesla service documentation, Braun instruction cards, Swiss editorial
Adult-learning basisAdults resist being taught and accept being referenced. A manual grants authority over the material instead of asking for compliance.
Your ethosStrongest fit. A manual is a foundation document. This is what the companies you worked at never wrote.

Where your story lives

Foreword

Four companies. Four industries. The same crack in the floor.

Tesla. Expedia. Highmark. Barneys. Different decades, different problems, one thing in common: teams moving fast on top of foundations nobody had ever been taught. Not because people weren't smart. Because no one had explained the fundamentals in language that made sense to the people doing the work. I spent my career in that gap. AI is about to repeat it, faster and wider than any system I've watched fail. So this is the manual I wish those teams had been handed.

Trade-off. The most distinctive and the most defensible, and the only one where your credibility is structural rather than decorative. Risks reading cold or clinical if the writing doesn't stay warm, and the numbering demands the taxonomy actually holds up, which is now the case.

B

One Thing at a Time

The interface disappears. One idea per screen, set enormous, with essentially no chrome. Effortless in the literal sense: there is never a decision to make.

InspirationApple onboarding, iOS Focus, Oak and Headspace, gallery wall labels
Adult-learning basisCognitive load theory, taken to its conclusion. Every visible control is working memory spent on the interface instead of the idea.
Your ethosWeakest fit as-is. Needs a deliberate moment for the story, because there is no furniture to hang it on.

What I'd do

A for the shell, B for the learning moment. Field Manual gives the product its authority, its index and its home for your story. Then when a unit opens, the manual gets out of the way and the analogy goes full-screen and enormous, the way B does it.

Why not CIt is the safe answer. Nothing about it would make the reviewer's skeptical colleague think this was made by someone with a point of view, and that is the thing you are actually selling.
Why not B aloneIt has nowhere to put a glossary, a profession picker, or four companies' worth of hard-won credibility.
What A+B buysStructure when orienting, silence when learning. The manual is the foundation; the analogy is the moment. That is also, precisely, your thesis.
