Orchestration & Workflows: The Conductor

One AI task is handy. The real power is chaining several into a workflow that runs start to finish, with you conducting. Here's how that works.

In this module

  • What orchestration and a “workflow” actually mean
  • The conductor-and-orchestra analogy
  • How to chain your first multi-step workflow

The problem

One task is nice. A whole workflow is the unlock.

You've used AI for single tasks: draft this, summarize that. But your real work isn't one task, it's a sequence: pull the notes, summarize them, draft the email, format it. Doing each by hand still leaves you stitching the steps together.

What if the steps connected on their own, with you still calling the shots?

The core analogy

The Conductor

A single musician is fine. An orchestra, dozens of players, instruments, and timings, becomes music only because a conductor coordinates them: brings in the strings, then the brass, keeps everyone on tempo.

Orchestration is you conducting several AI steps (and tools) so they play together as one workflow. You don't play every instrument. You direct the piece.

An “agent” is one capable player. A “workflow” is the piece they perform. Orchestration is how you, the conductor, bring each part in, in the right order, to the right effect.

How it actually works

How orchestration works

Orchestration (in plain words: coordinating several AI steps into one flow) chains tasks so the output of one becomes the input of the next.

A workflow is the sequence

For example: “take these meeting notes, pull out the decisions and action items, draft a recap email, and list my follow-ups.” One request, several coordinated steps.

Agents and tools are the players

Some steps just think (summarize); some use tools or connectors (check your calendar, prepare a draft). The conductor decides who plays, and when.

Where this lives

Built-in workflow and automation features, custom assistants that follow a multi-step recipe (Level 2), or tools like Zapier and Make for connecting apps. Same idea everywhere: define the steps once, run them on demand.

What this means for you

Conduct your first workflow

1. Map the sequence first, on paper

Write the steps you do by hand for one recurring task. That list is the workflow.

2. Chain two steps to start

Have AI do step one, then feed step two: “summarize this, then draft an email from the summary.” Build up from there.

3. Stay the conductor

Review at the hand-offs, especially before anything sends. You set the tempo and call the cues.

Try it this week

Take a 3-step task you do by hand and ask AI to run it as one connected flow. Notice where it needs your cue.

One takeaway

Orchestration is conducting an orchestra: you chain several AI steps into one workflow and direct the piece. You don't play every instrument, you bring each in at the right time.
